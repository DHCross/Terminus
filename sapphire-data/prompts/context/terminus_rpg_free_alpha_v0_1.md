# TERMINUS RPG - ALPHA

Version: Alpha Draft 0.1
Status: Functional Alpha Packet
Use: Table play and systems testing

Design Philosophy

## Current Engine

The Terminus engine uses paired Skill and Threshold dice. This system eliminates Ability/Discipline pools, default d6 spreads, and large ablative defense pools. Conflict loops resolve without to-hit rolls or passive target numbers. Players encounter the world through fiction-first mechanics rather than simulation language.

## Ancestry

### Lattice Inheritance

Standard d4 through d12 die ladder.
Front-loaded character creation.
Character identity acts as a central play object.
Order Abilities evolved from older feat systems.
Battle maps as table interfaces.

### GWSD Inheritance

Ground, Will, Shift, and Drift define active Scene states.
The Scene Card provides the Guide's source of truth.
Fiction as executable structure.
Dynamic pressure replaces static encounter design.

# Keeping Terminus Playable

Terminus is not an excuse to replace adventure with lore. Most sessions should still be about immediate problems: reach the crossing, recover the witness, clear the barrow, escort the body, find the missing child, stop the bad bargain, survive the wrong street. The deeper truth of Terminus gives these problems shape, but it should not smother them. That is the guardrail you need. It protects the game from its own intelligence.

1. The Quiet Day is the emotional anchor.
2. The point is not an endless rupture. The point is that ordinary life should be possible, beautiful, and worth defending. That gives the setting heart. Without it, Terminus becomes just another grim machinery-world.
3. Routine is civic magic without calling it magic.
4. Markets, bells, transit schedules, court procedures, ward inspections, burial customs, and repeated prayers are not just color. They are the world's stabilizing habits. That is one of the most distinctive ideas in the whole setting.
5. Ruptures should begin as mundane wrongness.
6. Not a portal opens and demons attack by default. Better: a clerk signs tomorrow's document, a ferry arrives with drowned passengers, a street gets longer the harder you try to leave, an elevator opens on the wrong floor. That keeps the weird close to ordinary life.
7. The Accord prevents the concept from becoming lonely.
8. The Sixfold Accord is the practical answer to why are these people together? It gives you the Guardian's Guild function without copying it. The key is that an Accord House is infrastructure, not a cozy hero club. Some Houses are corrupt, underfunded, compromised, or willing to burn cells.
9. Orders are world institutions first, character options second.
10. Seekers, Breakers, Wardens, Rivals, Brokers, Shades are not just classes. They are civilization's learned responses to instability. That keeps them from feeling like reskinned fighter/wizard/rogue slots.
11. The deep secret should stay deep.
12. NotebookLM was right to warn that Terminus itself should not eclipse regular adventure. The campaign does not need to be about discovering we live in a simulation. That can be endgame truth, deep cosmology, or Guide-facing substrate. Most play should still be about crossings, ruins, bargains, monsters, missing people, corrupt Houses, salvage, debt, fear, weather, and bad choices.
13. Regular adventures are not a compromise.
14. This is huge. A three-room barrow, an escort job, a monster hunt, a salvage mission, a local feud, a ferry disaster - all of those belong. The Terminus layer should sharpen those adventures, not replace them with cosmology lectures.
15. Civic ruin is a better aesthetic than generic dark fantasy.
16. Dead transit authority, ruined public works, broken seals, route maps, black archive cards, inspection forms, terminal platforms, cold brass, bone paper, dried oxblood. That's much more specific than dark parchment and skulls.
17. Pantheons can exist, but they should serve lived practice.
18. Gods should have symbols, sacred colors, taboos, tools, places, legal customs, funeral rites, oath forms, and public rituals. Don't start with cosmic charts. Start with what a peasant, clerk, soldier, or Warden does differently because of the god.
19. Emotional pacing matters.
20. If every scene is grim, the game flattens. Hamlet's Hit Points / Kobold-style advice points toward alternation: fear and hope, loss and discovery, pressure and relief. Terminus needs victories, warmth, humor, strange beauty, and quiet competence.

# 1. Quick Guide to the Setting of Terminus

What the World Is Called
The people of the world do not call it Terminus. To them, the world is simply Aurel, the Concord Lands, the Known Marches, the Old Continuum, or something similarly lived-in and historical. Terminus is not common speech. It is the buried name of the final architecture beneath reality - the secret frame that explains why the world holds together, why it fails, and why certain places stop obeying ordinary rules. For now, I'd use a plain-world name with civic weight: Aurel. Short, old, easy to say, and flexible enough to hold many cultures. So: The game is Terminus. The world is Aurel. Terminus is the secret under Aurel.

Aurel at a Glance
Aurel is a dark fantasy world built on the remains of a luminous high-fantasy age. It is not a ruined wasteland. Its cities still ring bells, open markets, register births, hold trials, bless bridges, bury the dead, inspect wards, run transit lines, and keep civic calendars. People still marry, argue, sell bread, forge tools, gossip, worship, and sleep. That ordinary life matters.
In Aurel, routine is not just habit. Routine helps stabilize reality. When markets open in the correct order, when trains arrive at the appointed bell, when priests repeat the old civic blessings, when judges speak verdicts in proper form, when burial rites are completed, when lamps are lit at dusk - the world becomes more reliable. When those routines fail, reality begins to slip. That failure is called a Rupture.

The Quiet Day
The central emotional idea of the setting is the quiet day. A quiet day is a day when no one has to become heroic just to survive. The bakery opens. The tram arrives. The child returns from school. The watchman finishes his route. The dead stay buried. The bridge carries people both ways. The bell rings once. The Orders do not exist because the world wants adventure. They exist because sometimes the quiet day breaks.

Rupture
A Rupture is not merely a monster attack or a magical curse. A Rupture is a systemic failure. Something that was supposed to keep reality stable begins misfiring. Examples: A street grows longer the harder one tries to leave it. A clerk signs a document dated tomorrow. A court verdict changes depending on who enters the room first. A ferry arrives with passengers who drowned last week. A signal bell rings twice, and the crowd repeats the same motion. A bridge supports only people walking north. A dead transit line accepts passengers. A door opens into the wrong year.
Most people experience Rupture as fear, inconvenience, superstition, bureaucracy, or local disaster. They do not know the deeper truth. They only know that something has gone wrong and ordinary institutions are not enough.

The Sixfold Accord
Aurel's answer to Rupture is the Sixfold Accord. The Accord is not an adventurers' guild. It is closer to an emergency field institution, a licensing body, a court of last resort, and a dangerous public service all at once. It exists because no single kind of expert can safely answer a Rupture. A scholar may know what is happening but cannot hold a collapsing ward. A soldier may hold a bridge but cannot understand why it rejects certain names. A negotiator may calm a crowd but cannot open a sealed route. A thief may enter the impossible room but cannot legitimize what was found there.
So, the Accord licenses mixed cells. Player characters are members, contractors, debt-bound agents, provisional responders, disgraced specialists, or emergency recruits attached to an Accord House. The simple campaign premise: You are an Accord cell assigned to a live instability.

The Six Orders
The Orders are not just classes. They are the recognized ways civilization has learned to respond when reality fails.

Seekers
Seekers reveal what is hidden, buried, false, forgotten, sealed, or waiting. They are investigators, field scholars, relic auditors, omen-readers, and truth-pullers. In Aurel, hidden knowledge is not academic. It can save a district. Their danger: revealing truth before the world can survive it.

Breakers
Breakers force openings. They breach walls, wards, lies, legal deadlocks, sealed roads, doomed machines, and impossible rooms. Some are liberators. Some are terrifying. Their danger: opening what should have remained sealed.

Wardens
Wardens hold collapse at bay. They keep bridges standing, doors shut, crowds alive, wards intact, and enemies from crossing the line. Sometimes one more minute is enough to save a city. Their danger: preserving a doomed structure past mercy.

Rivals
Rivals live inside contests. They turn chases, duels, wagers, comparisons, races, public measures, and formal challenges into structured outcomes. In a fragile world, formal conflict is often better than war. Their danger: making every situation into something someone must lose.

Brokers
Brokers turn obligation into motion. They manage debts, bargains, faction pressure, passage rights, treaties, patronage, and dangerous promises. They make pressure negotiable. Their danger: converting every human bond into leverage.

Shades
Shades work through access, concealment, misdirection, and reversal. They do not merely hide. They move through places where attention fails. They go where force, law, and negotiation cannot. Their danger: becoming indistinguishable from the breach they exploit.

Accord Houses
Accord Houses stand at places where trouble repeats: terminal crossings, old Rupture sites, border cities, dead districts, sealed roads, transit hubs, ruined public works, and frontier courts. A House may contain maps, sealed evidence, cells, contracts, field gear, dead-letter vaults, witnesses, route permissions, old reports, and emergency shelter. But an Accord House is not automatically noble. Some are corrupt. Some are captured by local politics. Some are underfunded. Some treat their cells as expendable. Some hide the truth to preserve order. The Accord makes campaigns easy to start, but it does not make morality easy.

Magic
Magic in Aurel is not primarily fireworks. Magic is permission, law, oath, infrastructure, and controlled exception. A Working might make a door refuse names not entered in the civic ledger. A bridge might carry only those under a valid compact. A bell might make a crowd hear one voice clearly. A ward might hold fire behind a chalk line. A contract might keep a road open until sunset. Magic asks: Who has permission to do this? What changes? How far does it reach? How long does it hold? What does the scene allow afterward? Magic is powerful because it changes what reality accepts.

Species and Stability
Species in Aurel are not decorative ancestry. Each has a relationship to stability. Humans are adaptable builders of new institutions after old ones fail. High Alfar remember old structures, civic memory, and stable high magic. Their tragedy is that memory is not authority. Deep Alfar know buried systems, undercities, foundations, and pressures beneath the visible world. Wild Alfar thrive where routine is already loosening: edges, fractures, unstable borders, and broken routes. Stoneborn remember pressure as architecture. They change slowly and break slowly.

Religion and Pantheons
Aurel needs gods, but gods should enter play through lived practice first. Not cosmic exposition. A god should be known by symbols, sacred colors, taboo tools, feast days, legal oaths, funeral rites, bridge blessings, market customs, and household rituals. Pantheons should behave like political families: allied, fractured, regional, jealous, compromised, interpreted differently by sects and cities. A priest in Aurel may matter less because they cast spells and more because they know the correct rite to make a bridge accept the dead.

The Look of Aurel
Aurel should not look like generic dark fantasy parchment. It should look like civic ruin over old beauty. Use: black stone, cold brass, bone paper, dried oxblood, ash gray, terminal platforms, inspection forms, transit signage, broken seals, route maps, oath ledgers, field packets, celestial grids treated as infrastructure. Avoid: decorative flowers, romantic gothic filigree, cozy guild whimsy, generic skull-and-parchment gloom, bright heroic fantasy polish. The world was beautiful before it became unstable. That beauty should still be visible under soot, law, and ruin.

What Adventures Look Like
A Terminus adventure does not need to reveal the secret of reality. Most adventures should still be clear and playable: Escort a witness. Recover a relic. Clear a barrow. Investigate a repeating street. Survive a broken crossing. Negotiate with a corrupt House. Find a missing child. Stop a bridge from rejecting mourners. Hunt a creature nesting inside an old signal tower. Recover a sealed report from a district that repeats yesterday. The deeper truth gives these adventures shape, but it should not smother them. The secret of Terminus can wait years. The game begins with a quiet day breaking.

The Secret
The hidden truth of the setting is Terminus. Terminus is the final architecture beneath Aurel. It is why routine matters, why Rupture happens, and why reality behaves like it can lose permissions. But this is not the default campaign objective. The characters do not need to solve Terminus to matter. They respond to lived failures. They protect people, exploit openings, make bargains, expose lies, hold lines, and survive places where ordinary life has stopped working. The secret gives the world depth. It does not replace adventure.

One-Paragraph Pitch
Aurel is a dark fantasy world where routine keeps reality stable. Markets, bells, transit lines, court rituals, burial customs, ward inspections, and civic prayers are not just culture; they are patterns that hold the world together. When those patterns fail, reality ruptures. Streets repeat, doors open into wrong rooms, signals misfire, and ordinary institutions lose authority. Player characters are members of recognized Orders working through the Sixfold Accord - not adventurers looking for trouble, but field responders sent where the quiet day has broken. The game is called Terminus because beneath all of this lies a deeper truth, but the people of Aurel know only the failures they must survive.

# 2. Core Premise

Terminus presents a dark fantasy setting where reality strains under systemic failure. Characters respond when stable existence falters. Three principles govern the game: Routine stabilizes reality; Rupture signals systemic failure; and Orders respond when institutions fail.

---

# 3. The World Under Strain

The world of Terminus maintains stability through routine. Habits - transit schedules, market orders, factory whistles, and civic blessings - form stabilizing patterns. When healthy, the system accepts life without resistance. A Rupture begins when routine fails: a repeated footstep, a signal changing out of order, or a door opening into the wrong room.
Orders respond when law, craft, violence, or scholarship cannot stabilize a situation. When a single method fails to contain a Rupture, multiple Orders respond in concert.

# 6. Character Creation

1. Choose Species: Select Human, HighAlfar, DeepAlfar, WildAlfar, or Stoneborn.
2. Choose Order: Select Seeker, Breaker, Warden, Rival, Broker, or Shade.
3. Choose Approach: Define your primary expression of pressure.
4. Choose Signature: Select your character's defining item.
5. Assign Skill Dice: Distribute 5 build steps among Skills. Each step raises a Skill from d4 up one rank (Max d12).
6. Derive Thresholds: Set circles based on Skill ranks.
7. Select Order Abilities: Pick three.
8. Choose Equipment: Select a primary weapon/tool and secondary item.
9. Define Background: Add a name, background sentence, and objective.

# 7. Species

## Human

Humans dominate many civic structures through adaptability.
Flexible Training: Once per scene, you can step up one Skill roll by one die rank if the action aligns with your Order.

## HighAlfar

HighAlfar anchor stable high magic and formal civic memory.
Old Law: When you act within a formal institution or ancient civic structure, you can step up one Willpower or Exert roll.

## DeepAlfar

DeepAlfar perceive hidden layers and the pressures beneath visible reality.
Under-Sight: Once per scene, ask the Guide what hidden pressure, ward, or flaw exists nearby.

## WildAlfar

WildAlfar thrive in edge-zones where stability already weakens.
Fracture Step: When the scene is unstable, you can step up one Agility or Avoid roll.

## Stoneborn

Stoneborn endure in harsh environments and hold old structural memory.
Hard Memory: Once per scene, ignore the first lost Endure circle from environmental pressure or crushing force.

# 8. Orders

Orders define field roles and play identities. They respond to Ruptures that stable systems cannot resolve.

## Seeker

Seekers reveal what is hidden, false, or sealed.
Order Abilities:
Weak Point: After you study a target, you can name one way to pressure it.
Trace Source: When you examine a pressure or wound, you can ask where it originated.
Bring to Light: You can reveal a hidden path, ward, or motive if you reach evidence of it.

## Breaker

Breakers force openings and ruptures in stagnant systems.
Order Abilities:
Breach Point: When you damage a barrier, you can open a temporary passage.
Overrun: When you win with Force, you can drive the target back or break its position.
Break the Tool: You can target a weapon or mechanism instead of the wielder.

## Warden

Wardens hold the line where collapse is in motion.
Order Abilities:
Hold the Line: You can take an ally's Threshold loss if you interpose.
Anchor Point: Choose a boundary. Until you move, enemies cannot easily force it open.
Brace Against It: When you Endure, you can protect one nearby person from that same pressure.

## Rival

Rivals leverage duels, chases, and public pressure.
Order Abilities:
Call the Contest: You can propose terms for a contest. Acceptance binds both sides to those terms.
Outpace: When timing matters, you can force a direct contest before the opponent moves.
Turnabout: When an opponent fails against you, you can immediately claim leverage.

## Broker

Brokers turn obligations and faction pressure into action.
Order Abilities:
Call in Favor: You can introduce one plausible contact or minor resource into the scene.
Make Terms: Propose terms that change what sides are willing to risk.
Turn the Room: Reveal leverage to shift one hesitant NPC toward action.

## Shade

Shades utilize concealment, misdirection, and reversal.
Order Abilities:
Unmarked Entry: You can enter a point of entry without becoming the scene's focus.
False Trail: Leave evidence that redirects suspicion or pursuit.
Turn It Back: Redirect an opponent's deception back toward them.

# 9. Conflict and Pressure

Terminus resolves conflict without to-hit rolls. Actions always matter; the target decides the outcome's resolution.

## Core Exchange

1. Acting side chooses a Skill.
2. Responding side chooses a Threshold.
3. Both sides roll.
4. The higher roll takes control.
5. The loser routes the consequence through their chosen Threshold.

# 11. Scenario: The Timed Crossing

Guide Read-Aloud:
The crossing is loud and exact. A bell sounds. Signals change in sequence. The tram glides in just as market awnings snap into place. No one stops. No one collides. Then the north signal changes out of order. One line of pedestrians steps forward; the other does not stop. The same bell rings again.

## Scene Card

Ground: Crowded but orderly. Signals and habit govern movement.
Will: The system preserves routine. Timing begins to desynchronize.
Shift: Interrupting the pedestrian jumps the loop to a signal post. Damage to a post breaks crowd confidence.
Drift: Signals change out of sequence. Crowd compresses. Duplicate tram becomes solid.
